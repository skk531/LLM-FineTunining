A savings account is one of the most fundamental banking products offered by financial institutions. It allows individuals to deposit money and earn interest over time. Savings accounts are designed for individuals who want to keep their money safe while earning a modest return. The interest rate on savings accounts typically ranges from 3% to 7% per annum, depending on the bank and the account balance maintained.

A current account, also known as a checking account, is primarily designed for businesses and individuals who perform high-frequency transactions. Unlike savings accounts, current accounts do not earn interest on deposits. However, they offer features such as overdraft facilities and unlimited transactions, making them suitable for commercial purposes. Businesses use current accounts to manage day-to-day operational expenses.

A fixed deposit (FD) is a financial instrument where you deposit a lump sum amount for a fixed period and earn interest at a predetermined rate. The interest rates on fixed deposits are generally higher than savings accounts and are locked in at the time of investment. Fixed deposits can range from 7 days to 10 years, and premature withdrawal may attract a penalty. FDs are considered one of the safest investment options in banking.

A recurring deposit (RD) is a savings product that allows customers to deposit a fixed amount every month for a predetermined period. At maturity, the customer receives the principal along with the accumulated interest. Recurring deposits are ideal for individuals who want to save regularly but do not have a lump sum to invest. The interest rates on RDs are similar to fixed deposits and are compounded quarterly.

Know Your Customer (KYC) is a mandatory process for banks to verify the identity of their customers. KYC helps banks prevent fraud, money laundering, and other financial crimes. The documents required for KYC include a government-issued photo ID such as an Aadhaar card, PAN card, passport, or voter ID, along with address proof. Banks are required by the Reserve Bank of India to complete KYC for all account holders.

A PAN (Permanent Account Number) card is a ten-digit alphanumeric identifier issued by the Income Tax Department of India. PAN is mandatory for all financial transactions above a certain threshold, including opening bank accounts, investing in mutual funds, and filing income tax returns. Banks require PAN card details for account opening and for reporting transactions to the tax authorities.

Net banking, also known as online banking or internet banking, allows customers to perform banking transactions through the internet. Customers can access their account details, transfer funds, pay bills, apply for loans, and manage their investments through the bank's website or mobile application. Net banking is available 24/7 and provides a convenient alternative to visiting a branch.

Mobile banking is the use of a mobile device to perform banking transactions. Mobile banking applications allow customers to check account balances, transfer funds, pay utility bills, recharge mobile phones, and apply for banking products. Most banks offer dedicated mobile apps for iOS and Android platforms. Mobile banking is secured through multi-factor authentication including OTP (One-Time Password) verification.

NEFT (National Electronic Funds Transfer) is an electronic payment system that allows transfer of funds between bank accounts across India. NEFT transactions are processed in batches and typically take a few hours to complete. NEFT is available on all days including bank holidays and can be done through net banking, mobile banking, or bank branches. There is no minimum transaction limit for NEFT, and the charges vary by bank.

RTGS (Real-Time Gross Settlement) is an electronic payment system for high-value fund transfers. Unlike NEFT, RTGS transactions are settled in real time and on a gross basis. The minimum transaction limit for RTGS is Rs. 2 lakh. RTGS is suitable for large business transactions where immediate transfer of funds is required. RTGS is available from 7 AM to 6 PM on weekdays and 7 AM to 1 PM on Saturdays.

IMPS (Immediate Payment Service) is a 24/7 instant interbank electronic fund transfer service. IMPS allows transfer of funds instantly at any time, including holidays. The maximum transaction limit for IMPS is Rs. 5 lakh. IMPS can be initiated through mobile banking, net banking, or ATMs. IMPS uses the Mobile Money Identifier (MMID) and mobile number or account number and IFSC code for transfer.

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) is a real-time payment system developed by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI). UPI allows customers to link multiple bank accounts to a single mobile application and perform fund transfers using a Virtual Payment Address (VPA) or UPI ID. UPI transactions can be done 24/7 and are instant. The daily transaction limit for UPI varies by bank but is generally Rs. 1 lakh.

A credit card is a payment card that allows the holder to borrow funds from the issuing bank to make purchases. Credit card holders receive a monthly statement and are required to pay the outstanding amount by the due date to avoid interest charges. Credit cards offer features such as reward points, cashback, travel benefits, and insurance coverage. The credit limit is determined by the bank based on the applicant's income and credit history.

A debit card is a payment card linked to the cardholder's bank account. When a purchase is made using a debit card, the amount is directly deducted from the linked bank account. Debit cards can be used for online and offline transactions, ATM withdrawals, and contactless payments. Debit cards are generally safer than carrying cash and are accepted at most merchant establishments.

A personal loan is an unsecured loan provided by banks and financial institutions for personal use such as medical expenses, travel, wedding, or home renovation. Personal loans do not require collateral and are approved based on the borrower's creditworthiness, income, and repayment capacity. Interest rates on personal loans range from 10% to 24% per annum, and the repayment tenure is typically 1 to 5 years.

A home loan, also known as a mortgage, is a secured loan used to purchase or construct a residential property. The property itself serves as collateral for the loan. Home loans are offered at both fixed and floating interest rates. The repayment tenure for home loans can be up to 30 years. Banks typically finance up to 80-90% of the property value, and the borrower must contribute the remaining amount as a down payment.

An auto loan or car loan is a secured loan used to purchase a new or used vehicle. The vehicle serves as collateral for the loan. Auto loans are offered at competitive interest rates and can have repayment tenures of 1 to 7 years. Banks typically finance up to 85-90% of the vehicle's on-road price. The interest rate on auto loans depends on the borrower's credit score, income, and the type of vehicle being purchased.

An education loan is a financial product that helps students fund their higher education expenses including tuition fees, accommodation, books, and other related costs. Education loans are offered for both domestic and international educational programs. The moratorium period for education loans typically extends until 6-12 months after course completion or employment, whichever is earlier. Interest rates on education loans range from 8% to 15% per annum.

EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) is the fixed payment amount made by a borrower to the lender at a specified date each calendar month. EMI consists of both principal and interest components. In the initial period of loan repayment, the interest component is higher, and it gradually decreases as the loan is repaid. The EMI amount depends on the loan amount, interest rate, and repayment tenure.

A credit score is a numerical representation of an individual's creditworthiness, calculated based on their credit history. In India, CIBIL (Credit Information Bureau India Limited) is one of the major credit bureaus that maintains credit scores. A CIBIL score ranges from 300 to 900, with scores above 750 considered excellent. A high credit score increases the chances of loan approval and may result in better interest rates.

Credit utilization ratio is the percentage of available credit that is currently being used. It is calculated by dividing the total credit card balance by the total credit limit. A credit utilization ratio below 30% is considered healthy and has a positive impact on the credit score. High credit utilization indicates financial stress and can negatively affect the credit score.

Overdraft is a credit facility that allows account holders to withdraw more money than what is available in their account. The excess amount withdrawn is treated as a loan and attracts interest charges. Overdraft facilities are commonly offered on current accounts for businesses. Some banks also offer overdraft facilities against fixed deposits and other securities as collateral.

A mutual fund is a professionally managed investment vehicle that pools money from multiple investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, and other securities. Mutual funds are managed by Asset Management Companies (AMCs) and are regulated by SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India). Mutual funds offer different schemes such as equity funds, debt funds, hybrid funds, and index funds to suit different investor profiles.

SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) is a method of investing in mutual funds where a fixed amount is invested at regular intervals, typically monthly. SIP allows investors to benefit from rupee cost averaging and the power of compounding. SIP investments can be started with as little as Rs. 500 per month. SIPs are a disciplined approach to long-term wealth creation and are suitable for investors with a long investment horizon.

Public Provident Fund (PPF) is a long-term savings instrument backed by the Government of India. PPF offers a guaranteed return with tax benefits under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act. The current interest rate on PPF is approximately 7.1% per annum, compounded annually. The lock-in period for PPF is 15 years, with the option to extend in blocks of 5 years. Partial withdrawals and loans against PPF are allowed after specific periods.

National Savings Certificate (NSC) is a fixed-income investment scheme backed by the Government of India, available through post offices and authorized banks. NSC has a maturity period of 5 years and offers a fixed interest rate. Investments in NSC qualify for tax deductions under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act. NSC is a low-risk investment option suitable for conservative investors seeking guaranteed returns.

GST (Goods and Services Tax) is applicable on banking services in India. GST is charged on banking fees, processing charges, and other financial services provided by banks. The current GST rate on most banking services is 18%. GST is included in the total amount charged to the customer's account for services such as ATM transaction failures, cheque bounce charges, and account maintenance fees.

TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) is a mechanism used by the Income Tax Department to collect tax at the source of income. Banks deduct TDS on interest income earned from fixed deposits and other savings instruments. TDS is deducted at 10% if the interest income exceeds Rs. 40,000 per year (Rs. 50,000 for senior citizens). Customers can submit Form 15G/15H to prevent TDS deduction if their total income is below the taxable limit.

IFSC (Indian Financial System Code) is an 11-character alphanumeric code used to identify bank branches in India. IFSC is required for electronic fund transfers through NEFT, RTGS, and IMPS. The first four characters represent the bank code, the fifth character is always '0', and the last six characters represent the branch code. IFSC can be found on the cheque book, passbook, or the bank's website.

MICR (Magnetic Ink Character Recognition) is a 9-digit code printed on cheques using magnetic ink. MICR is used to identify the bank and branch for processing cheque transactions. The first 3 digits represent the city code, the next 3 digits represent the bank code, and the last 3 digits represent the branch code. MICR is printed below the account number on the bottom of a cheque leaf.

A cheque is a negotiable instrument issued by the account holder to authorize payment to a specific person or entity. Cheques can be of different types including bearer cheques, order cheques, crossed cheques, and account payee cheques. A cheque is valid for 3 months from the date of issuance. Stale cheques (cheques presented after 3 months) are returned by the bank and need to be reissued.

Cheque bounce occurs when a cheque presented for payment is returned by the bank due to insufficient funds or other discrepancies. Cheque bounce is a punishable offense under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act. The bank charges a penalty fee for cheque bounce, and the drawer may face legal action. It is important to ensure sufficient funds in the account before issuing a cheque.

Banking fraud includes unauthorized transactions, phishing, identity theft, card skimming, and online scams. Banks have implemented multiple security measures including two-factor authentication, transaction alerts, and fraud monitoring systems to protect customers. Customers should never share their PIN, CVV, OTP, or login credentials with anyone. In case of suspected fraud, customers should immediately contact the bank's customer care and block their cards.

Phishing is a type of cybercrime where fraudsters impersonate legitimate organizations to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information such as bank account details, passwords, and OTPs. Phishing attacks are typically carried out through emails, SMS messages, or fake websites that look identical to official banking portals. Banks never ask for account details, passwords, or OTPs through email or phone calls.

Card skimming is a technique used by fraudsters to capture card information from ATMs or point-of-sale terminals. Criminals install skimming devices on ATMs that read the magnetic stripe data of debit or credit cards. Customers should inspect ATMs for any suspicious devices before using them and cover the keypad while entering the PIN. Banks have introduced EMV chip cards that are more secure than magnetic stripe cards.

Loan prepayment refers to the repayment of a loan amount before the scheduled maturity date. Prepayment can be either partial (paying a portion of the outstanding principal) or full (paying the entire outstanding amount). Prepaying a loan reduces the total interest outgo and helps become debt-free faster. Some banks charge a prepayment penalty, while others offer zero prepayment charges for floating-rate loans.

Loan restructuring is a process where the terms of an existing loan are modified to provide relief to borrowers who are facing financial difficulties. Loan restructuring may involve extending the repayment tenure, reducing the interest rate, or converting unpaid interest into principal. Banks undertake loan restructuring to avoid loan defaults and NPAs (Non-Performing Assets). The Reserve Bank of India provides guidelines for loan restructuring.

NPA (Non-Performing Asset) refers to a loan or advance that has not generated income for the bank for a period of 90 days or more. When a borrower fails to make interest or principal payments for 90 days, the loan is classified as an NPA. Banks are required to maintain provisions for NPAs, which affects their profitability. Banks take legal actions to recover NPAs through mechanisms such as SARFAESI Act and Debt Recovery Tribunals.

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is the central bank of India, established on April 1, 1935. RBI is responsible for regulating and supervising the banking sector, formulating monetary policy, managing foreign exchange, and issuing currency notes. RBI sets the repo rate, reverse repo rate, and other key policy rates that influence interest rates across the banking system. RBI also regulates payment and settlement systems in India.

Repo rate is the rate at which the Reserve Bank of India lends money to commercial banks. When the repo rate is increased, borrowing becomes expensive for banks, which in turn raises interest rates for customers. When the repo rate is decreased, borrowing becomes cheaper, and banks may lower their lending rates. Changes in the repo rate affect EMIs on loans, especially floating-rate loans.

SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) is the regulatory authority for securities markets in India. SEBI regulates stock exchanges, brokers, mutual funds, and other market participants. SEBI aims to protect the interests of investors and promote the development of the securities market. SEBI has established regulations for disclosure requirements, insider trading, and market manipulation to ensure fair and transparent markets.

NSE (National Stock Exchange) and BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange) are the two primary stock exchanges in India. NSE and BSE provide a platform for buying and selling securities such as shares, bonds, and derivatives. The NIFTY 50 index tracks the performance of 50 large-cap companies listed on NSE, while the SENSEX tracks 30 companies listed on BSE. Investors can participate in the stock market through Demat accounts and trading accounts.

A Demat account (Dematerialized account) is an account that holds securities such as shares, bonds, and mutual funds in electronic form. Demat accounts eliminate the need for physical share certificates and make it easier to trade and transfer securities. A Demat account is operated by depositories such as NSDL (National Securities Depository Limited) and CDSL (Central Depository Services Limited). A trading account linked to the Demat account is required to buy and sell securities.

Foreign exchange (Forex) refers to the conversion of one currency into another. Banks offer foreign exchange services including currency exchange, travel cards, foreign currency drafts, and wire transfers. The exchange rate between two currencies is determined by the forex market and fluctuates based on supply and demand. Banks add a margin to the market exchange rate when providing forex services to customers.

SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) is a global messaging network used by banks to securely transmit financial information and conduct international money transfers. Each bank participating in the SWIFT network is assigned a unique SWIFT code (also known as BIC - Bank Identifier Code). SWIFT codes are used to identify banks for international wire transfers and correspondent banking.

A nominee is a person designated by the account holder to receive the account balance and assets in the event of the account holder's death. Nomination is a safety measure that ensures smooth transfer of assets to the designated person without legal complications. Customers can add or change nominees for their bank accounts, fixed deposits, and other financial products at any time. Nomination does not override a will.

Senior citizens are offered preferential banking services including higher interest rates on fixed deposits (typically 0.25% to 0.75% higher than regular rates), free or concessional service charges, and dedicated banking counters. Senior citizens above 60 years of age are eligible for these benefits. Banks also offer doorstep banking services for senior citizens and differently-abled customers who cannot visit branches.

A joint account is a bank account held by two or more individuals. Joint accounts can be operated on either an "Either or Survivor" (EOS) basis where any one account holder can operate the account independently, or a "Joint" basis where all account holders must sign for transactions. Joint accounts are commonly used by spouses, business partners, and family members. In the event of death of one account holder, the funds can be accessed by the surviving account holder(s).

Interest rates on loans can be either fixed or floating. A fixed interest rate remains constant throughout the loan tenure, providing predictability in EMI payments. A floating interest rate is linked to a benchmark rate such as MCLR (Marginal Cost of Funds based Lending Rate) or RBI's repo rate, and changes periodically as the benchmark rate changes. Floating rate loans can result in lower or higher EMIs depending on market conditions.

MCLR (Marginal Cost of Funds based Lending Rate) is the minimum lending rate below which banks are not permitted to lend, as mandated by the RBI. MCLR is determined based on the bank's cost of funds and other factors. All variable-rate loans sanctioned after April 2016 are linked to MCLR. When the RBI changes the repo rate, MCLR-linked loans eventually get repriced at the next reset date.

Annual Percentage Rate (APR) is the annual cost of credit expressed as a percentage of the loan amount. APR includes not just the interest rate but also processing fees, insurance premiums, and other charges. APR provides a comprehensive measure of the true cost of a loan and allows borrowers to compare loan offers from different lenders. Borrowers should always compare APR rather than just the nominal interest rate when evaluating loans.

Account dormancy occurs when a bank account has had no customer-initiated transactions for a period of 2 years. A dormant account can be reactivated by submitting a written request to the bank along with KYC documents. Banks are required to transfer the balance of dormant accounts to the RBI's Depositor Education and Awareness (DEA) Fund after 10 years of inactivity. These funds can be claimed by the account holder or their nominee at any time.

Digital wallets or e-wallets are electronic versions of a physical wallet that store money digitally. Popular digital wallets in India include Paytm, PhonePe, Google Pay, and Amazon Pay. Digital wallets allow users to make payments at merchant establishments, transfer money to other wallet users, and pay utility bills. Digital wallets are regulated by the RBI and must comply with KYC requirements for higher transaction limits.

Contactless payment technology allows customers to make payments by simply tapping their card or smartphone near a contactless-enabled payment terminal. Contactless payments use NFC (Near Field Communication) technology for secure and convenient transactions. Most banks have replaced older debit and credit cards with contactless-enabled cards. Contactless payments are typically limited to Rs. 5,000 per transaction without requiring a PIN.

Balance transfer is a facility that allows credit card holders to transfer the outstanding balance from one credit card to another card with a lower interest rate. Balance transfers can help reduce the interest burden on credit card debt. Banks typically offer promotional interest rates on balance transfers for a limited period. It is important to compare the balance transfer fees and post-promotional interest rates before availing this facility.

Loan against securities allows borrowers to obtain a loan by pledging financial assets such as shares, mutual funds, fixed deposits, insurance policies, and bonds as collateral. The loan amount is typically a percentage of the market value of the pledged securities. Loan against securities is a cost-effective borrowing option as the interest rates are generally lower than personal loans. If the value of pledged securities falls below a certain level, the borrower may receive a margin call.
